There is something about the forest that resists computation. Not because it is complex — though it is — but because its complexity is of a kind that feels alien to the grid.

## the procedural and the organic

When we talk about procedural generation, we often mean the imitation of organic forms through algorithms. Trees that branch according to L-systems. Terrain shaped by Perlin noise. But the forest does not follow an algorithm. It follows itself.

> "The tree which moves some to tears of joy is in the eyes of others only a green thing that stands in the way."

Consider the work of [Aristid Lindenmayer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristid_Lindenmayer), whose L-systems gave us a grammar for plant-like growth. His formal language could describe branching patterns with remarkable fidelity. And yet — something is always missing.

## what the algorithm leaves out

The algorithm leaves out the wind. It leaves out the deer that rubbed its antlers against the bark. It leaves out the mycorrhizal network that connects root to root beneath the soil, exchanging nutrients in a slow underground commerce.

These are not details. They are the thing itself.
