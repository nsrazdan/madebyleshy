## What is an interval?

An interval is the distance between two notes, measured in semitones (half steps). Every melody, chord, and harmony you hear is built from intervals. Understanding them is the foundation of music theory.

On a guitar, one fret equals one semitone. Moving from fret 3 to fret 5 on any string is a distance of 2 semitones — a **major 2nd**.

## The twelve intervals

Within a single octave, there are twelve possible intervals:

| Semitones | Name | Sound |
|-----------|------|-------|
| 0 | Perfect Unison | same note |
| 1 | Minor 2nd | tense, close |
| 2 | Major 2nd | a whole step |
| 3 | Minor 3rd | sad, dark |
| 4 | Major 3rd | bright, happy |
| 5 | Perfect 4th | open, strong |
| 6 | Tritone | unstable, dissonant |
| 7 | Perfect 5th | powerful, hollow |
| 8 | Minor 6th | bittersweet |
| 9 | Major 6th | warm, sweet |
| 10 | Minor 7th | bluesy |
| 11 | Major 7th | dreamy, jazzy |
| 12 | Perfect Octave | same note, higher |

## How to find intervals on the fretboard

The simplest way: count frets. If two notes are on the same string, the number of frets between them equals the number of semitones.

When notes are on different strings, you need to account for the tuning offset between strings. In standard tuning (EADGBE), each string is 5 semitones higher than the one below it — except the B string, which is only 4 semitones above the G string.

## Practice

The best way to learn intervals is by ear and by feel. Try the [fretboard intervals exercise](/music-practice/exercises/fretboard-intervals) to test your knowledge — you'll be shown two notes on the fretboard and asked to identify the interval between them.

As you practice, try to associate each interval with a feeling or a song you know. The perfect 5th is the opening of many rock power chords. The minor 3rd is the distance between the first two notes of a minor chord.

## Why this matters

Once you can recognize intervals instinctively, you can:
- **Transcribe music by ear** — hear a melody and know the notes
- **Improvise** — navigate the fretboard by sound, not just shapes
- **Understand chords** — a chord is just a stack of intervals
- **Communicate with other musicians** — "go up a minor 3rd" is universal
